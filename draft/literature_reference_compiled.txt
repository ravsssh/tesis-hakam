Sitasi: Kung, J. J., Carverhill, A. P., & Mcleod, R. H. (2010). Indonesia’s stock market: Evolving role, growing efficiency. Bulletin of Indonesian Economic Studies, 46(3), 329–346. https://doi.org/10.1080/00074918.2010.522503
The document discusses the evolution and increasing efficiency of Indonesia's stock market, particularly following a series of reforms and deregulation measures implemented from 1987 onwards.
Overview of Indonesia's Stock Market Evolution
The article discusses the transformation of Indonesia's stock market from a period of inactivity to a significant player in the country's financial system, particularly following reforms initiated in the late 1980s. It highlights the growing efficiency and importance of the stock market relative to the banking sector.
Historical Context of Indonesia's Stock Market
The Jakarta Stock Exchange (JSX) was re-opened in 1977 after two decades of inactivity.
Initial growth was limited, with only 24 stocks listed from 1977 to 1984.
The market was primarily government-driven, with little public participation and low per capita incomes.
Impact of Economic Reforms on Stock Market
Economic reforms began in December 1987, known as Pakdes I, aimed at revitalizing the stock market.
Deregulation measures included lifting prohibitions on foreign ownership of shares and enhancing private sector involvement.
The stock market's activity surged post-reforms, with the Jakarta Composite Index rising from 80 in 1987 to 637 by 1996.
Changing Dynamics Between Banks and Stock Market
The stock market's role grew significantly, surpassing bank loans in total value by 2004.
The 1997-98 financial crisis highlighted the risks of over-reliance on banks, prompting a shift towards diversifying financial intermediation.
The government’s deregulation efforts allowed the stock market to play a more substantial role in funding and investment.
Efficiency of the Stock Market
The study examines the effectiveness of technical trading rules, such as moving averages and trading range breakouts.
Results indicate that investors could achieve above-average returns using these strategies, particularly in the earlier sub-period (1988-1996).
The efficiency of the market improved over time, with a notable decline in the predictability of returns in the later period (1999-2007).
Methodology for Analyzing Stock Market Performance
The analysis utilized daily return data from the Jakarta Composite Index, excluding the crisis years of 1997-98.
Two technical trading strategies were employed to assess their effectiveness in generating returns.
One-tailed hypothesis tests were conducted to compare mean returns from trading signals against unconditional mean returns.
Summary of Trading Strategies and Results
Moving average rules in the 1988-96 period yielded high mean daily returns, averaging 0.00199, significantly above the unconditional mean of 0.00087.
In contrast, the 1999-2007 period showed diminished returns, with mean daily returns for buys averaging 0.00121, closely aligning with the unconditional mean.
Trading range breakout rules also demonstrated strong performance in the earlier period but underperformed in the later period, with average returns for buys at 0.00518.
Conclusion on Stock Market's Role in Economic Development
The stock market's contribution to Indonesia's economic development has increased dramatically since the late 1980s.
Policy changes have facilitated private sector participation and foreign investment, enhancing market efficiency.
The reforms have laid a strong foundation for the stock market, although returns have stabilized closer to average levels in recent years.
ASEAN Finance Ministers Meetings
Joint ministerial statements from various ASEAN countries highlight financial cooperation.
Meetings took place in locations such as Prague, Vientiane, Yangon, and Hanoi between 1999 and 2005.
Key Financial Literature
Notable works include Greenspan's lessons from global crises and Levine & Zervos on stock markets and economic growth.
Other significant studies focus on banking deregulation in Indonesia and the role of non-bank financial institutions.
Major Reform and Deregulation Measures
The 1987 Pakdes package allowed stock prices to move according to market forces.
Key reforms included the establishment of the Indonesian Central Securities Depository in 1997 and the merger of stock exchanges in 2007.
